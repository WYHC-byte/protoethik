The Ouroboros Dilemma:
Entropy Decay in Recursively Trained LLMs

An empirical analysis of Mode Collapse under recursive Knowledge Distillation protocols.
November 26, 2025   •   E. Vance, et al.
Abstract

As the reservoir of high-quality human-generated text approaches depletion, the machine learning paradigm is shifting towards training on synthetic data generated by Large Language Models (LLMs). While Knowledge Distillation (KD) effectively compresses capabilities from Teacher to Student models, recursive application of this process ($M_i \rightarrow \text{Data} \rightarrow M_{i+1}$) introduces a phenomenon we term Model Autophagy. In this work, we demonstrate that recursive training without external entropy injection leads to a measurable collapse in the probability distribution tails, resulting in models that exhibit low perplexity but high "blindness" to rare concepts. We propose a Hybrid Entropy Injection (HEI) framework that utilizes rejection sampling based on fractal dimension analysis to sustain model variance over generations.

1. Introduction: The Saturation of Human Priors

The scaling laws of Deep Learning (Kaplan et al., 2020) rely heavily on the assumption of independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) data acquisition. However, recent projections indicate that high-quality public text data will be exhausted by 2026 (Villalobos et al., 2022). This impending "Data Wall" necessitates a shift towards Synthetic Data. While techniques like Self-Instruct have shown promise in alignment, they pose existential risks when used for pre-training. This paper investigates the thermodynamic consequences of closed-loop AI training systems.

2. Theoretical Framework: Distillation as Compression

Knowledge Distillation (KD) can be mathematically formalized as the minimization of the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the teacher's soft targets and the student's probability distribution. The objective function $\mathcal{L}_{KD}$ is defined as:

$\mathcal{L}_{KD} = (1 - \alpha) \mathcal{L}_{CE}(y, \sigma(z_s)) + \alpha \tau^2 \mathcal{L}_{KL}(\sigma(z_s/\tau), \sigma(z_t/\tau))$

Where $z_s$ and $z_t$ are logits of the student and teacher, $\tau$ is the temperature, and $\alpha$ balances the hard (ground truth) and soft losses. As $\tau \to \infty$, the gradient approximates the matching of logits, effectively transferring the "dark knowledge" or inter-class relationships.

algorithms/kd_loss.py 
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def distillation_objective(student_logits, teacher_logits, labels, T=4.0, alpha=0.7):
    """
    PyTorch implementation of KD Loss with Temperature Scaling.
    Args:
        T (float): Temperature. Higher T softens the distribution, exposing tail classes.
        alpha (float): Weighting for the soft target loss.
    """
    # 1. KL Divergence Loss (Soft Targets)
    # log_softmax is required for KLDivLoss in PyTorch
    p_s = F.log_softmax(student_logits / T, dim=1)
    p_t = F.softmax(teacher_logits / T, dim=1)
    
    loss_kl = F.kl_div(p_s, p_t, reduction='batchmean') * (T ** 2)
    
    # 2. Cross Entropy Loss (Hard Targets / Ground Truth)
    loss_ce = F.cross_entropy(student_logits, labels)
    
    # 3. Weighted Combination
    return loss_kl * alpha + loss_ce * (1. - alpha)

3. The Phenomenon of Model Collapse

When a model is trained on data generated by a previous version of itself (or a similar model), the variance of the data distribution shrinks. Since generative models effectively sample from the mean of their training distribution to maximize likelihood, they systematically under-sample the tails (rare events).

Proposition 1: Variance Decay

"Given a sequence of models $M_0, M_1, ... M_n$ where $M_{i+1}$ is trained on $\mathcal{D}_i \sim M_i$, the differential entropy $H(\mathcal{D})$ is strictly decreasing, i.e., $H(\mathcal{D}_{i+1}) < H(\mathcal{D}_i)$. This results in a convergence towards a delta function centered on the most probable tokens."

This decay is particularly dangerous in Visual Large Language Models (V-LLMs) and multi-modal transformers (e.g., Swin Transformer architectures), where "averaging" visual features results in textures losing high-frequency detail and logical spatial relationships dissolving—a phenomenon akin to "jpeg artifacts" in cognitive reasoning.

We simulated 5 generations of recursive training using a LLaMA-3-8B base. We tracked Perplexity (PPL) and Unique N-gram usage (a proxy for creativity).

4. Methodology: Hybrid Entropy Injection (HEI)

To mitigate collapse, we propose a Hybrid Diet. While synthetically generated reasoning chains (CoT) improve logic (as seen in DeepSeek-R1 and o1), they must be grounded by "Entropy Anchors"—pristine human data that preserves the irregular, non-linear nature of reality.

Our HEI framework utilizes a discriminator network trained to identify "synthetic artifacts"—statistical watermarks left by LLMs. Data is only admitted to the training set if it passes a Burstiness Threshold, ensuring it contains sufficient novelty.

5. Conclusion & Future Work

The "Ouroboros" of AI training AI is not sustainable in a closed loop. Our findings suggest that while Knowledge Distillation is a powerful compression technique, it cannot create new information entropy. Future work will focus on Active Learning strategies where the Student model explicitly requests human annotation only for high-uncertainty regions, optimizing the "Human-in-the-Loop" bandwidth.
